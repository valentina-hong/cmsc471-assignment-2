ASSIGNMENT 2 WRITE-UP

This visualization shows Greenhouse Gas Emissions in various contexts and statistics of the over-time reduction or growth of emissions. The data is sourced from The Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), an international forum for democratic and free market economies that convenes to generate and pursue policy for global economic growth and well-being. This data uses all of the member countries’ emissions data from 1988 to 2023, found through their National Inventory Submissions to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change. This visualization seeks to investigate further into these changes and discover how countries have been progressing in their emissions reduction over time, especially in recent years.

White Hat Visualization

The first section of the visualization is the White Hat section. The three visualizations in this section aim to report the data as accurately and cleanly as possible. When creating these visualizations, our team focused on three key aspects to accomplish truthful data reporting: context, encodings, and annotations. By focusing on making these three aspects as interpretable as possible, all three of our visualizations became much more powerful and explanatory.

The first visualization is a bar graph showing the per capita greenhouse gas emissions in 2023, ranking the countries from highest to lowest per capita emissions. This visualization uses full context by providing all 44 member countries of the OECD in the visualization alongside groupings of emissions (high, mid, and low) thresholds and emphasizing this through the color encodings. The colors are specifically meant to evoke good and bad, with red being tied to higher emissions per capita, while green is given to the countries with lower emissions per capita. Blue is given to countries in the middle, or with about neutral emissions, compared to their peers. The exact value is shown via tooltips and side annotations on the bar, with each value displayed alongside the legend at the top to explain the color encoding, providing sufficient context for the entire visualization. This visualization helps to take a full look at the extremes while still being able to contextualize that extreme against peers, which establishes a contextual basis of how extreme those countries' emissions per capita really are and a reasonable threshold for what they should be working towards with policy.

The second visualization takes similar data but instead shows trend lines, providing a time-series perspective on emissions reduction between 2014 and 2023. The trend lines show a very large contextual scope around various events and time within that span, such as the COVID-19 quarantine, that would see an emissions reduction. The larger contextual timespan helps show the story of declining emissions for all 8 countries since 2014. Although there is a larger dip ~2020 for many countries, they were already on a decreasing trend beforehand, showing COVID-19 quarantine had little influence on emissions reduction per capita. Contrasting colors were chosen to help keep each line distinct from the other, making it easy to track each nation using the legend given at the top of the visualization. In addition, there are tooltips available for each year to show the exact emissions per capita for each given country.

The final White Hat visualization is a diverging bar chart meant to show the trend line data more concisely, showing the overall growth or reduction of emissions per capita from 2014 to 2023, as shown in the left-hand title. This keeps the encodings from the previous bar chart consistent, using green to show reduction and red to show growth, which is positive and negative for society, respectively. Further annotation is given through both the value at the end of the bar as well as the tooltips, showing both the exact ton reduction value as well as the percent change in emissions per capita from the 2014 value to the 2023 value. This helps provide more context on what countries and regions should seek to change policy to promote emissions reduction, rather than focusing on raw output values, which don’t tell the full story.

Black Hat Visualization

YThe second section of the visualization is the Black Hat section. This section seeks to use the same dataset, but generates wrong insights due to various mistakes made while generating the visualizations of the data. When thinking about how to misrepresent the data, our team came up with three main ideas on how to do so: incorrect axes, cherry-picked data, and minimizing time-series scope. Using these three methods, our team developed three visualizations that misrepresent the data as strongly as possible, leading to incomplete insights drawn from them. These visualizations are meant to mirror the White Hat section to show how the same data could easily be transformed when not handled correctly or even maliciously.

The first visualization uses truncated axes on a bar graph to overemphasize the difference in emissions per capita between countries, causing the differences between the top emitters per capita to be much larger than they really are. Viewers can easily conclude from this visualization that Australia, Canada, and Kazakhstan are the world's top emitters and seem almost unbounded in how much greenhouse gas they are emitting. This graph also fails to provide a full context for the selected countries, not specifying that this only accounts for OECD member countries. By eliminating this context as well, the other 100+ countries not included in the OECD are also put into perspective as lower emitters than the countries given on this bar graph. Both of these malicious changes to the visualization inflate the emissions of Australia to a very large degree, which could misdirect policy changes to target Australia despite its decrease in emissions, as seen in the White Hat visualizations.

The second visualization focuses on cherry-picking only specific countries on the diverging bar chart to limit the entire scope of climate reductions. By showing only countries with minimal reductions and one country with gains, it eliminates the true story of widespread emissions reduction. The title perfectly emphasizes this point and is written to inflame the viewer’s feelings about climate change and emissions reduction. In addition, this visualization specifically chooses to use the country with the largest amount of growth to best contrast the minimal growth from the other countries. This reinforces the concept of stagnation by countries in their emissions reduction efforts. Finally, the graph also chooses to omit more information about the scope and time frame from which this data is pulled. The tooltips also don’t give any further information on this point, instead opting to only include the value of change, as well as the phrase “Most countries that improved are removed.” While this phrase helps keep the visualization more honest, it doesn’t provide any further context on this group, while hiding in the tooltips rather than being a disclaimer on the larger visualization.

The final Black Hat visualization is a sliced version of the trend lines from the White Hat section, this time only including the years of 2019 to 2023. In this span, the trend lines instead stagnate much more, even showing growth for some countries. This visualization cherry picks specific countries from the data that show high amounts of emissions in addition to stagnation or growth in some periods. This removes the larger story of a strong overall reduction prior to 2019 that continues on into the 2020s, and instead replaces it with stagnation and failure to improve in the 2020s, with only 2019 as a previous baseline to reference. In addition, this reinforces the previous narrative that Australia is above and beyond the biggest contributor to greenhouse gas emissions by over 10 tons compared to other countries. This comparison can be made due to the cherry-picking of countries, such as Canada and New Zealand, two comparable emitters, which are left out of the trend lines. The tooltips follow a similar format to the previous visualization, containing more context about things that were omitted, without elaborating on the additional context that makes the White Hate visualizations successful.

Comparison

Listed below are the specific design choices that distinguish the White Hat and Black Hat visualizations, focusing on contrasting elements that improve or diminish readability.

White Hat

  • Y-axis starts at zero
  • All countries shown
  • Full year range
  • Neutral, factual title
  • Per-capita units stated

Black Hat

  • Truncated axis exaggerates
  • Only worst performers shown
  • Recent uptick decontextualised
  • Accusatory framing
  • No baseline for comparison

Extra Credit Features

On this project, our team worked towards multiple Extra Credit Features. The first is the development of 3 White Hat and 3 Black Hat visualizations for a total of 6 visualizations. Our team was able to create three larger visualizations to best explain and visualize the data as truthfully as possible, following our principle guidelines to make 3 effective visuals in the White Hat section of the project. We also honed in on specific techniques in the Black Hat section to develop cohesive storytelling elements that aren’t true based on the entire data, but could easily be concluded by each of the three visualizations in that section.

Secondly, on our White Hat 2023 ranking visualization, we include multiple sorting and filtering options to change and dynamically adapt the visualization. The user can sort by lowest or highest values first, in addition to changing the range of countries that are included, ranging from all 90 in the dataset to only the top 10. This feature strongly enhances the usability and insights obtainable by allowing the user to filter and access the exact range that they want to look at when evaluating policy and regions to target policy.

Thirdly, all 6 of our visualizations contain smoothed animation in addition to tooltips to provide additional context for the user to read and take in. These made all of our visualizations much more interactive and easy to view, making the overall user experience much stronger. Our team wanted to incorporate as much of a good viewing experience as possible with our White Hat visualizations alongside easy-to-obtain insights for the OECD to generate policy towards.
